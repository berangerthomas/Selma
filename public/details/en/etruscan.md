# Etruscan Alphabet

## Overview
The Etruscan alphabet was adapted from a **Western Greek** variant around **700 BCE** by the Etruscan civilization of northern and central Italy. It served as the direct model for the Latin alphabet, making it a crucial link in Western writing history.

## The Etruscans
The Etruscans were a sophisticated civilization who dominated the Italian peninsula before the rise of Rome. Their language remains only partially understood — it is a **language isolate**, unrelated to Indo-European languages.

## Alphabet Structure
The Etruscan alphabet had 26 letters in its earliest form (though the number varied by period and region). Several letters were retained from Greek but represented sounds absent in Etruscan — these were eventually repurposed by the Romans.

## The Latin Connection
When the early Romans adopted the Etruscan alphabet:
- They **kept** letters they needed (A, B, C, D, E, etc.)
- They **dropped** letters for sounds they lacked (e.g., Θ, Φ, Χ)
- They **added** new ones (G, later)
- They **reversed direction**: Etruscan was right-to-left; Latin became left-to-right

## Legacy
Because Etruscan gave Latin its letters, and Latin gave them to virtually every European language, Etruscan is arguably the **most influential extinct alphabet** in history — shaping the way over 3 billion people write today.
