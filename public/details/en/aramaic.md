# Aramaic Script

## Overview
Aramaic was the lingua franca of the ancient Near East for over a millennium. Its script — derived from Phoenician — became the parent of the most diverse script family in the world.

## Historical Importance
- **Imperial Aramaic** was the administrative language of the Achaemenid Persian Empire (550–330 BCE), used from Egypt to India
- **Biblical Aramaic** appears in sections of Daniel and Ezra
- Jesus of Nazareth is believed to have spoken a Galilean dialect of Aramaic

## Script Descendants
The Aramaic script is a prolific parent in the writing systems DAG:

| Script | Region | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Hebrew** (square) | Levant | Active (co-parent with Phoenician) |
| **Nabataean** | Arabia | Extinct → ancestor of Arabic |
| **Syriac** | Mesopotamia, Syria | Active liturgically |
| **Sogdian** | Central Asia | Extinct → ancestor of Uyghur, Mongolian |
| **Brahmi** | South Asia | Extinct → ancestor of 100+ modern scripts |

## Writing Direction
Like Phoenician, Aramaic is written **right to left**, an orientation inherited by Arabic and Hebrew.

## Modern Survival
Aramaic survives today in:
- **Syriac** — liturgical language of Eastern Christian churches
- **Jewish Neo-Aramaic** — spoken by some Jewish communities
- **Turoyo and Assyrian Neo-Aramaic** — spoken in Iraq, Syria and diaspora communities
