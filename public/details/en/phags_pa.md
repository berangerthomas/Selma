# Phags-pa Script

## Overview
Phags-pa (/'Phags-pa) was commissioned by **Kublai Khan** and created by the Tibetan monk **Drogön Chögyal Phagpa** in **1269 CE** to serve as the unified official script of the **Mongol Empire**. Its name means "Exalted" in Tibetan.

## Purpose
Kublai Khan needed a script that could write all the empire's languages equally well — Mongolian, Chinese, Tibetan, Sanskrit and beyond. Phags-pa, derived from Tibetan, was designed as a universal phonetic script.

## Unique Vertical Direction
Phags-pa is written **top to bottom** like Mongolian (its sibling script), but it is an abugida derived from Tibetan (which is horizontal). This makes it typologically unusual.

## Official Use
From 1269 to roughly 1368 (fall of the Yuan Dynasty), Phags-pa appeared on:
- Imperial seals and coins
- Government documents
- Paiza (travel passes used on the Silk Road)
- Stone inscriptions across China, Mongolia and Korea

## The Hangul Connection
Korean scholars, including possibly those who helped create **Hangul** (1443 CE), are known to have studied Phags-pa. The systematic phonological structure of Hangul — where letter shapes encode articulatory features — may owe a conceptual debt to Phags-pa's design principles.
