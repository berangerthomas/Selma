# Nabataean Script

## Overview
The Nabataean script was the writing system of the **Nabataean Kingdom** (c. 200 BCE–106 CE), centred at **Petra** (modern Jordan). It descended from Aramaic and is the direct ancestor of the **Arabic script**.

## The Nabataean Kingdom
The Nabataeans were Arab merchants who controlled the lucrative incense trade routes connecting Arabia, the Mediterranean and India. Their capital, Petra, was carved into rose-red sandstone cliffs and remains one of the wonders of the ancient world.

## Script Characteristics
- An **abjad** (consonants only), right-to-left
- Letters began connecting (ligatures), a key step toward Arabic's cursive style
- The letter shapes became increasingly cursive and simplified over time

## The Bridge to Arabic
The transformation from Nabataean to early Arabic is well-documented in inscriptions. Key transitional texts include:
- The **Namara inscription** (328 CE) — possibly the earliest known Arabic text in Nabataean-derived script
- The **Zebed inscription** (512 CE) — one of the first texts clearly identified as Arabic script

## Legacy
When Nabataean script became Arabic, it inherited an empire: the Arabic alphabet now serves **1.8 billion** speakers across 25+ countries.
