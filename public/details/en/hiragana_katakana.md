# Hiragana and Katakana

## Overview
Hiragana (ひらがな) and Katakana (カタカナ) are two **syllabic scripts** (*kana*) derived from Kanji, developed in Japan during the **9th century CE**. Together they form a complete phonetic system for Japanese.

## Origins

| Script | Derived from | Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Hiragana** | Kanji | **Cursive** simplification of full characters |
| **Katakana** | Kanji | **Angular** simplification of character components |

Examples:
- Hiragana あ ← 安 (*an*) cursive
- Katakana ア ← 阿 (*a*) component

## Structure
Each kana represents a **syllable** (strictly, a mora):
- 46 basic hiragana + 46 katakana (same sounds, different shapes)
- Diacritics (*dakuten* ゛, *handakuten* ゜) modify voicing: か (ka) → が (ga)
- Combined forms for sounds like きゃ (kya), びょ (byo), etc.

## Modern Use

| Script | Primary uses |
|---|---|
| **Hiragana** | Grammatical particles, verb endings, native Japanese words, children's texts |
| **Katakana** | Loanwords (*koohii* コーヒー = coffee), scientific/technical terms, emphasis, sound effects |

## Cultural Note
Learning hiragana and katakana (typically in the first year of Japanese school) is the gateway to literacy in Japan. Each syllabary can be mastered in a few weeks — making the phonetic layer of Japanese far more accessible than the kanji layer.
