# Armenian Script

## Overview
The Armenian script was created in **405 CE** by the monk **Mesrop Mashtots**. It was primarily modelled on the **Greek** alphabet (letter order, vowel representation) and likely also drew on **Syriac** for some letterforms and the concept of a complete phonological representation.

## Creation
According to tradition, Mashtots received divine inspiration for the letter shapes. In practice, he developed 36 letters (later expanded to 38) precisely calibrated to the phonological system of Classical Armenian — including both vowels and the rich consonantal inventory of Armenian.

## Unique Characteristics
- One of the few alphabets designed by a **known, historically documented individual**
- Letters were designed to have **no resemblance to Greek or Syriac** (preserving Armenian cultural identity)
- Written **left to right**
- The alphabet has remained **remarkably stable** for 1,600 years

## The Armenian Bible
The immediate purpose of the script was to translate the **Bible** into Armenian. The Armenian translation, completed c. 430 CE, is sometimes called the "Queen of Translations" for its literary quality.

## Modern Armenian
Two standard forms exist today:
- **Eastern Armenian**: spoken in Armenia and Iran
- **Western Armenian**: spoken in the diaspora (Turkey, Lebanon, USA)

Both use the same script with minor orthographic differences.
