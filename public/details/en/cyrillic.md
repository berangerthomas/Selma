# Cyrillic Script

## Overview
Cyrillic was created in the **9th–10th century CE** by Bulgarian scholars (traditionally attributed to disciples of Saints Cyril and Methodius). It drew its letterforms primarily from **Greek** uncial script while also incorporating letters from **Glagolitic** for sounds absent in Greek.

## Design Philosophy
Cyrillic was designed to be an improved successor to Glagolitic — retaining the phonological completeness of Glagolitic (which could represent all Slavic sounds) while using the more familiar and prestigious Greek letter shapes.

## Classical Cyrillic Alphabet (Old Bulgarian)
The original alphabet had ~44 letters. Modern Russian uses **33**.

## Languages Written
Cyrillic is the official script of:
- **Russian** (~150 million speakers)
- **Ukrainian** (~45 million)
- **Bulgarian**, **Serbian**, **Macedonian**
- **Mongolian** (Outer Mongolia, since 1941)
- **Kazakh**, **Kyrgyz**, **Tajik**, **Uzbek** (partially)
- Dozens of minority languages of Russia and Central Asia

## Total Speakers
Approximately **250 million** people use Cyrillic as their primary script.

## Soviet Influence
The USSR systematically introduced Cyrillic for all Soviet peoples in the 1930s–40s, replacing Arabic, Latin and other scripts. Several post-Soviet states (Uzbekistan, Kazakhstan, Azerbaijan) are now transitioning back to Latin.
