# Hangul (Korean)

[![Hunminjeongeum, 1446](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Hunmin_jeong-eum.jpg)](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Hunmin_jeong-eum.jpg)
*The Hunminjeongeum ("The Correct Sounds for the Instruction of the People"), 1446. UNESCO Memory of the World.*

## Overview
Hangul (한글) is the official writing system of Korea, created in **1443 CE** by **King Sejong the Great** of the Joseon dynasty and introduced to the public in 1446 via the *Hunminjeongeum* document. It is considered one of the most scientifically designed alphabets ever created.

## A True DAG Node
Hangul has two distinct parents:
- **Hanja** (Chinese characters): conceptual influence, co-existence in the text system
- **Phags-pa**: structural and phonological design principles — letter shapes in Hangul may be partly inspired by Phags-pa's systematic articulation-based design

## Scientific Design
Hangul letters were designed to **visually encode articulation**:
- **ㄱ** (g/k): lateral profile of the tongue touching the velum
- **ㄴ** (n): tongue touching the alveolar ridge
- **ㅁ** (m): shape of closed lips
- **ㅅ** (s): shape of teeth
- **ㅇ** (ng): shape of the throat/circle

This phonological intentionality is unique in script history.

## Structure
Hangul is an **alphabet** where letters are grouped into **syllabic blocks**:
- 14 basic consonants + 10 basic vowels = 24 letters
- Combined into blocks of 2–4 letters per syllable
- Each block occupies the same visual square as a Chinese character — facilitating mixed Hanja/Hangul text

## Historical Suppression
The Joseon aristocracy (*yangban*) resisted Hangul, preferring Hanja as a marker of elite status. Hangul was long called *eonmun* ("vernacular script") dismissively. It became widespread only in the 20th century and was standardised after Korean independence.

## Today
Hangul is used exclusively in North Korea and predominantly in South Korea. Linguists widely regard it as one of the most elegant and systematic alphabets ever devised.
