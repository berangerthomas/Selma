# Akkadian Cuneiform

## Overview
Akkadian was a Semitic language spoken in ancient Mesopotamia that adopted Sumerian cuneiform around **2600 BCE**. It became the dominant language of the ancient Near East for over a millennium.

## Adaptation
The Akkadians borrowed cuneiform wholesale but adapted it for the phonological structure of their Semitic language. They retained many Sumerian logograms (used as *sumerograms*) while adding new syllabic readings.

## Two Dialects
Akkadian split into two major literary dialects:
- **Babylonian** — southern Mesopotamia, used for religious and literary texts
- **Assyrian** — northern Mesopotamia, used for royal correspondence and annals

## International Language
From approximately 1500 to 600 BCE, Akkadian served as the **lingua franca of the ancient Near East**, used in diplomatic correspondence from Egypt to Anatolia (the **Amarna Letters**, found in Egypt, are written in Akkadian).

## Decline
Akkadian was progressively replaced by **Aramaic** as the dominant regional language from around 600 BCE, and the last known Akkadian text dates to the 1st century CE.
