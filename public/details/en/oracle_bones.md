# Oracle Bone Script

[![Shang dynasty oracle bone](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/Shang_dynasty_inscribed_scapula.jpg)](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Shang_dynasty_inscribed_scapula.jpg)
*A turtle plastron inscribed with oracle bone script, Shang dynasty (c. 1200 BCE). National Museum of China.*

## Overview
Oracle Bone Script (*jiǎgǔwén* 甲骨文) is the earliest attested form of **Chinese writing**, dating from the **Shang dynasty** (c. 1200–1050 BCE). It represents the oldest continuous ancestor of the writing system used by over a billion people today.

## Medium
The script was inscribed on **turtle plastrons** (belly shells) and **ox scapulae** (shoulder blades) used for **divination**:
1. A question was posed to the ancestors or divine powers
2. Heat was applied to the bone/shell, causing cracks
3. The cracks were interpreted as the answer
4. The question, date, diviner's name and sometimes the outcome were inscribed around the cracks

## Nature of the Script
Oracle Bone Script is largely **logographic**:
- Pictographs: 日 (sun), 月 (moon), 山 (mountain), 木 (tree)
- Ideographs: 上 (above), 下 (below)
- Compound characters: 明 (bright) = 日 (sun) + 月 (moon)
- Phonetic components were already present in some characters

## Rediscovery
Oracle bones were rediscovered in **1899** when a Chinese scholar identified inscribed bones being sold as "dragon bones" in pharmacies. Systematic excavation of Anyang (the Shang capital) from 1928 onward yielded over **150,000 inscribed pieces**.

## Legacy
Oracle Bone Script evolved through the **Bronze Inscriptions**, **Seal Script**, **Clerical Script** and **Regular Script** into the characters used throughout East Asia today.
