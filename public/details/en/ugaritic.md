# Ugaritic

## Overview
Ugaritic is an alphabet (like Proto-Sinaitic/Phoenician) but written in **cuneiform wedges** (like Sumerian/Akkadian). It represents a unique convergence of two unrelated writing traditions.

## Origin
Discovered at **Ras Shamra** (ancient Ugarit, modern Syria) in 1929, Ugaritic dates to approximately **1400–1180 BCE**. Scribes in this cosmopolitan port city drew on both the alphabetic principle spreading from the Levant and the cuneiform medium dominant in Mesopotamia.

## Structure
The Ugaritic alphabet contains **30 signs**, each representing a single consonant — matching the abjad principle of Proto-Sinaitic. The letters are shaped as arrangements of cuneiform wedges rather than curvilinear pictographs.

## Why It Matters
Ugaritic provides critical evidence for the early stages of the alphabet. The Ugaritic texts include:
- The **Baal Cycle**, a major Canaanite mythological narrative
- Legal and administrative tablets
- The oldest known **alphabetical order** (*abecedary*), close to the modern ABC sequence

## Disappearance
Ugarit was destroyed around **1185 BCE** during the Bronze Age Collapse. Ugaritic died with it and was forgotten until modern excavations.
