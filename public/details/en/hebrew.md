# Hebrew Script

## Overview
The Hebrew script, also known as the **"square script"** (*Ktav Ashuri*), emerged from the convergence of Phoenician and Imperial Aramaic traditions. It shows a **dual heritage**: its square letterforms derive primarily from Aramaic, while its earlier paleo-Hebrew form descends directly from Phoenician.

## Two Phases
- **Paleo-Hebrew** (c. 900–135 CE): Used on coins, inscriptions and the Dead Sea Scrolls sections. Closely resembles Phoenician.
- **Square Hebrew** (from c. 3rd century BCE): Adopted from Aramaic during the Babylonian exile. Still used today.

## Structure
Hebrew is an **abjad** — 22 consonants, no vowels. The system of **niqqud** (diacritical dots and dashes for vowels) was added by Masoretic scholars in the 7th–10th century CE and is used today mainly in children's books, poetry and religious texts.

## Sacred Use
Hebrew is the language of the Torah, the Mishnah and most of the Hebrew Bible. It was **revived as a spoken language** in the 19th–20th centuries, an almost unique event in linguistic history.
