# Tibetan Script

## Overview
The Tibetan script was created in the **7th century CE** by the scholar **Thonmi Sambhota**, who was sent to India by Emperor Songtsen Gampo to devise a writing system for the Tibetan language. He adapted the Brahmi-derived script of Kashmir.

## Purpose
The primary motivation was to translate **Buddhist scriptures** from Sanskrit into Tibetan. The Tibetan Buddhist canon (*Kangyur* and *Tengyur*) comprises over 4,500 texts — one of the largest bodies of religious literature in the world.

## Structure
Like all Brahmi descendants, Tibetan is an **abugida**:
- 30 consonants, each with inherent /a/
- 4 vowel diacritics
- A complex system of **stacked letters** (consonant clusters written vertically)
- Written **left to right** in horizontal lines

## Spelling vs. Pronunciation
Tibetan orthography is famously conservative — spelling reflects Old Tibetan (7th c.) while pronunciation has shifted dramatically. The word written "bkra shis" is pronounced something like "Tashi".

## Scripts Derived from Tibetan
Tibetan is the parent of:
- **Phags-pa** (Mongolian Empire official script)
- **Lantsha** (ornamental Sanskrit script)
- Tibetan itself remains in use for **Tibetan, Dzongkha** (Bhutan's national language) and several Himalayan languages.
