# Georgian Script

## Overview
The Georgian script (*mkhedruli*) is one of the world's most distinctive alphabets. Traditionally attributed to the 5th century CE, it was likely influenced by or developed alongside **Armenian**, sharing that script's motivation: creating a vehicle for translating Christian scriptures into a local language.

## Three Georgian Scripts
Three distinct scripts exist for the Georgian language:

| Script | Name | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Asomtavruli** | "Capital letters" | Oldest (5th c.), stone inscriptions |
| **Nuskhuri** | "Minuscule" | Medieval ecclesiastical manuscripts |
| **Mkhedruli** | "Military/cavalry" | Modern everyday script (from 11th c.) |

## Structure
Georgian is a true **alphabet** (not an abjad or abugida):
- **33 letters** in modern Mkhedruli
- Represents both consonants and vowels explicitly
- Written **left to right**
- No capital letters in traditional Mkhedruli (though Mtavruli, a unicase script, is now used as capitals)

## Distinctive Aesthetics
Georgian letters are characterised by rounded, flowing strokes with distinctive tails and loops — bearing no visual relationship to any neighbouring script, reflecting deliberate cultural distinctiveness.

## Living Culture
Georgian is the official language of Georgia (~3.7 million speakers) and is spoken by diaspora communities in Russia, Iran and elsewhere. Georgia boasts a continuous literary tradition stretching back to the 5th century.
