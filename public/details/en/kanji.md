# Kanji (Japanese)

## Overview
**Kanji** (漢字, "Han characters") are Chinese characters adopted into the Japanese writing system. Japan borrowed Chinese script beginning around the **4th–5th century CE**, then adapted it profoundly to suit the very different phonological and grammatical structure of Japanese.

## The Adaptation Challenge
Chinese is largely **monosyllabic and isolating**; Japanese is **polysyllabic and agglutinative**. The mismatch led to:
- Using Chinese characters **logographically** (for meaning: 山 = *yama*, mountain)
- Using Chinese characters **phonetically** for Japanese syllables — which eventually gave rise to **Hiragana and Katakana**

## Modern Japanese Script System
Modern Japanese uses four scripts simultaneously:

| Script | Origin | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Kanji** | Classical Chinese | Content words, proper nouns |
| **Hiragana** | Kanji (cursive) | Grammatical elements, native words |
| **Katakana** | Kanji (angular) | Foreign words, emphasis, scientific names |
| **Rōmaji** | Latin | Romanisation, acronyms |

## Jōyō Kanji
The Japanese Ministry of Education maintains a list of **2,136 kanji** for general use (*jōyō kanji*). A high school graduate is expected to know all of them.

## Evolution
Kanji arrived via **Hanja** (Korean use of Chinese characters) and directly from China. The script continues to evolve: modern Japan uses simplified forms (*shinjitai*) that differ from traditional Chinese characters.
