# Sumerian Cuneiform

## Overview
Sumerian cuneiform is the **oldest attested writing system** in the world, emerging in Mesopotamia (modern-day Iraq) around **3100 BCE**. It takes its name from the Latin *cuneus* (wedge), referring to the wedge-shaped impressions made on clay tablets.

## Origins
The system originated as a record-keeping tool for the administration of the city of **Uruk** — tracking grain, livestock and trade goods. The earliest signs were pictographic; over centuries they became increasingly abstract.

## Structure
Cuneiform was originally logographic but evolved into a logosyllabic system: signs could represent entire words *or* syllables. Over 2,000 distinct signs were in use at the system's height.

## Spread
Cuneiform was adopted and adapted by dozens of languages over 3,000 years, including:
- **Akkadian** (Babylonian & Assyrian)
- **Elamite**, **Hittite**, **Hurrian**
- **Ugaritic** (an unusual alphabetic adaptation)

## Legacy
The **Epic of Gilgamesh**, the oldest known narrative poem, was written in cuneiform. The last known cuneiform tablet dates to **75 CE** — making cuneiform one of the longest-lived writing systems in history (over 3,000 years).
