# Modern Roman Alphabet

## Overview
The Modern Roman Alphabet is the direct descendant of Latin, extended with additional letters and diacritics to accommodate the phonological needs of hundreds of languages. It is the **most widely used writing system in the world**.

## Extensions and Additions
The alphabet grew from 23 classical Latin letters to a larger inventory:
- **J** — differentiated from I in the 16th century
- **U, W** — differentiated from V in the Middle Ages / Renaissance
- **Diacritics**: ä, ö, ü (German), é, è, ê (French), ñ (Spanish), ø (Danish), ç (French/Turkish), ł (Polish), etc.

## Languages and Scripts
| Language family | Scripts based on Roman |
|---|---|
| **Germanic** | English, German, Dutch, Swedish, Norwegian, Danish |
| **Romance** | French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian |
| **Slavic** | Polish, Czech, Slovak, Slovene, Croatian |
| **Turkic** | Turkish, Azerbaijani (officially), Uzbek |
| **African** | Swahili, Yoruba, Zulu, Hausa and many others |
| **East Asian** | Vietnamese, Pinyin (Chinese romanization) |

## Standardisation
The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) maintains multiple Latin script standards, allowing consistent encoding of all Latin-derived alphabets in digital systems.

## Statistics
- Used by approximately **3 billion** people natively
- Adopted as a secondary script by many more (pinyin, romanization systems)
- Supported by every modern operating system and digital platform
