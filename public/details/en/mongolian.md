# Mongolian Script

## Overview
The traditional Mongolian script (*Mongol bichig*) was created around **1204 CE** under Genghis Khan, adapting the Old Uyghur alphabet for the Mongolian language. It is one of the few scripts in the world still written in a **vertical, top-to-bottom** direction.

## Distinctive Features
- Written **vertically**, from top to bottom, columns from left to right
- An **alphabet** (not an abjad — it marks vowels explicitly)
- Letters connect within syllables
- 35 letters in its modern form

## History
The script flourished during the **Mongol Empire** (13th–14th c.) and the subsequent Mongolian states. It was the official script until the 20th century:
- **Outer Mongolia** (Mongolia) replaced it with Cyrillic in 1941
- **Inner Mongolia** (China) retained it and uses it officially to this day

## Revival
Mongolia officially began restoring the traditional script alongside Cyrillic in 1991 after independence. As of 2025, efforts to re-establish it as the primary script in Mongolia are ongoing.

## Notable Use
The **Secret History of the Mongols** (c. 1240), the oldest Mongolian literary work, was originally written in this script. It recounts the life of Genghis Khan and the origins of the Mongol nation.
