# Ancient South Arabian Script

## Overview
The Ancient South Arabian script (also called **Musnad**) was used in the kingdoms of the Arabian Peninsula — Saba (Sheba), Qataban, Hadramaut and Himyar — from approximately **900 BCE to 600 CE**.

## Origins
Like Phoenician, South Arabian derived from **Proto-Sinaitic**, but through an independent development path. It is not an ancestor of Arabic script — Arabic descended from Nabataean. South Arabian was a completely separate branch.

## Unique Direction
South Arabian was written in a distinctive way: it could be written **left to right or right to left**, and in **boustrophedon** (alternating directions line by line, like an ox ploughing a field).

## Alphabet
The South Arabian alphabet contains **29 consonants**, slightly more than Phoenician (22) or Arabic (28), reflecting different phonological distinctions.

## Famous Connection
The **Queen of Sheba** (*Bilqis* in Arabic tradition) reportedly ruled from Saba, whose inscriptions survive in South Arabian script. The script's cultural sphere also produced the temple city of **Marib** with its famous dam.

## Legacy
Ancient South Arabian gave rise to **Ge'ez (Ethiopic)**, carried across the Red Sea to the Horn of Africa, where it evolved into the script family used today for Amharic, Tigrinya and other Ethiopian and Eritrean languages.
