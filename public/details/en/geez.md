# Ge'ez Script (Ethiopic)

## Overview
The Ge'ez script, also called **Ethiopic**, descended from the Ancient South Arabian script and underwent a radical transformation: it became an **abugida** — the first in history. Each character represents a consonant with an inherent vowel, modified by diacritics for other vowels.

## From Abjad to Abugida
South Arabian was a pure consonantal abjad. When it was adapted for the Semitic languages of the Horn of Africa around the **4th–6th century CE**, vowel marking was systematically integrated. This was a major typological innovation.

## Structure
Ge'ez has **7 vowel orders** for each consonant:
- Each base consonant has 7 derived forms (one per vowel)
- Total: approximately **276 syllabic signs** in the traditional script

## Languages Written
The Ge'ez script today is used for:
- **Ge'ez** — classical liturgical language of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church
- **Amharic** — official language of Ethiopia (~60 million speakers)
- **Tigrinya** — official language of Eritrea (~7 million speakers)
- **Tigre**, **Bilen**, **Harari** and other Ethiopian languages

## Religious Importance
The Ethiopian Orthodox Church preserved the script through centuries of continuous use. The complete Bible in Ge'ez (*Haile Selassie* Bible) includes books not found in other Christian canons, such as the **Book of Enoch**.
