# From Latin to Romance Languages

## Introduction
Latin was the language of the Roman Republic and Empire. After Rome's fragmentation in the 5th century CE, spoken Latin evolved differently in each region — giving rise to the **Romance language family**. The writing system (the Latin alphabet) survived largely intact across all these languages.

## Alphabet Adaptations

Each Romance language adapted the Latin alphabet to fit its own phonology:

| Language | Key additions | Key distinctions |
|---|---|---|
| **French** | é, è, ê, ô, û, ç, œ, æ | Nasal vowels, silent finals |
| **Spanish** | ñ, ü (rare) | ¡ ¿ punctuation |
| **Portuguese** | ã, õ, â, ê, ô, ç | Nasal vowels, distinctive tilde |
| **Italian** | Very few — accent marks only | Closest to Classical Latin phonology |
| **Romanian** | ă, â, î, ș, ț | Cyrillic used until 19th c. |
| **Catalan** | l·l, ŀ, ç | Influenced by both French and Spanish |

## The Alphabet's Continuity

Despite 1,500 years of linguistic evolution, the Latin alphabet remains immediately recognisable across all Romance languages. A speaker of Classical Latin could read the letter shapes of a modern French newspaper — even if the words would be largely incomprehensible.

This is a testament to the **stability of writing systems** relative to spoken language: scripts are conservative artefacts, preserved by institutions (Church, state, schools) long after the spoken language has drifted.
